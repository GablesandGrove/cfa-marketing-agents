## 5 Non-Negotiables Assessment
- Direct: PASS — Opens with a dollar figure in the first word. No warm-up, no context-setting, no wasted sentence.
- Aggressive: PASS — "We built a machine to go get it." Declarative. No apology. The voice never flinches.
- Credible: PASS — "$26,000," "4 cold estimates," "close rate up 34%" — all traceable to approved proof sources. Gables & Grove named explicitly.
- Urgent: PASS — "That lead is still alive. Still deciding. Still waiting for someone to show up." Creates present-tense pressure without manufactured hype.
- Real: PASS — "We get busy. Three weeks pass. The job goes to someone else." This is how contractors talk to themselves at 5am. No translation required.

## 4-Question Test
1. 5am contractor read: PASS — The pacing is short paragraphs, punchy sentences. A contractor on a job site can read this in 90 seconds and feel called out in the right way.
2. Operator vs. consultant: PASS — "Here's what was happening at Gables & Grove before we fixed this" is textbook operator framing. Lived it, fixed it, telling you exactly what happened.
3. Generic agency sentence: CONDITIONAL PASS — One line needs attention: "No spam. No pressure. Just the right message at the right time, sent automatically, while we're on the job site." The phrase "just the right message at the right time" is soft and close to marketing-speak. Every email company uses a version of this. The rest of the sentence saves it — "while we're on the job site" is the differentiator that should lead. Flag for tightening (see Edits Required).
4. Andrew says it out loud: PASS — "We didn't hire a salesperson. We built a machine." That's a T-shirt. Andrew says it.

## Banned Words Check
CLEAN — No instances of: digital transformation, synergy, solutions provider, innovative, cutting-edge, revolutionary, empower, enable, streamline, leverage, holistic, ecosystem, robust, "we believe," "we're excited to," or unspecified "optimize."

## Proof Source Check
All claims traceable:
- "$26,000 in jobs we had already written off" — matches approved: "Recovered 4 cold estimates = $26K in 30 days"
- "4 cold estimates" — matches approved proof source
- "Close rate on quoted jobs up 34%" — matches approved proof source
- "Day 1. Day 3. Day 7. Day 14." — internal process, no external claim, no issue

## Voice Assessment: PASS
Lines that land perfectly:
- "$26,000. That's what was sitting in our quote folder doing nothing." — Ice cold open. The pause between the number and the sentence is doing real work.
- "That's not a sales problem. That's a process problem." — This is the insight line. It reframes the contractor's mental model in one sentence.
- "Count your last 30 days." — Turns it personal fast.
- "That gap is your number." — Ends the middle section with a gut punch.
- "We didn't hire a salesperson. We built a machine." — Best line in the post. Could headline a campaign.

Lines that need punching up:
- "No spam. No pressure. Just the right message at the right time, sent automatically, while we're on the job site." — Lead with the operator proof, not the reassurance. Reorder.

## Edits Required
**Line 30 rewrite** — Swap the order to lead with the credibility signal instead of the soft reassurance:

Current: "No spam. No pressure. Just the right message at the right time, sent automatically, while we're on the job site."

Revised: "We're on the job site when it sends. No spam. No pressure. The right message, at the right time, from a machine that doesn't forget."

- Credible: PASS — The cost breakdown table is the best credibility tool in the batch. Real numbers, specific assumptions, a live comparison. It doesn't claim — it calculates. The Gables & Grove attribution is explicit.

- Real: CONDITIONAL PASS — The post is slightly more polished than the others. The cost breakdown table shifts the register toward "business analysis" mode. This is acceptable for LinkedIn Core audience, but one section drifts into language a CFO would write, not a contractor. Flag below.

## 4-Question Test

And task execution is exactly what AI does better than humans." The insight is correct and useful, but the phrasing "task execution" repeated twice in formal syntax is slightly academic. Not a full flag — more a tone alert. Suggested revision below.
4. Andrew says it out loud: CONDITIONAL PASS — "Because the AI doesn't ask for a raise." — Andrew says this. "Your stack doesn't." — Andrew says this. The objection section, however, has one sentence too long to deliver naturally out loud. Flag below.

## Banned Words Check
CLEAN — No banned words detected.

## Proof Source Check
All claims traceable:
- "$60,000" overhead cut — approved: "$60K overhead cut at Gables & Grove"
- "Replaced 2 admin roles" — approved proof source
- "Running in 3 days" — approved: "Onboarding time: 2 weeks → 3 days"
- "$10,000–$15,000 one-time" and "$1,500–$3,000 monthly retainer" — these match CFA published service tiers exactly. Approved.
- "6–8 weeks to hire and train" — this is a general industry claim, not from approved proof sources. It is reasonable and defensible but not formally approved. Flag as NEEDS SOURCING or reframe as CFA experience.
- "Gone in 14 months (industry average turnover)" — same issue. Industry average claim without a cited source attached to the post. It may be accurate, but if challenged, there is no approved proof source to point to.

**UNVERIFIED CLAIMS:**

- "Gone in 14 months (industry average turnover)" — needs source citation or reframe as "In trades, they rarely last 18 months."

- "That's not relationship management. That's task execution. And task execution is exactly what AI does better than humans." — Too academic for the voice. Tighten.
- "A person shows up. A person answers the phone. But that person also calls in sick, gets poached by your competitor, and needs 6 weeks of training every time you scale." — The list runs long. Trim to two items for punch.

**1. Unverified industry claims — reframe three items in the cost breakdown:**

Current:
```
- 6–8 weeks to hire and train
- Gone in 14 months (industry average turnover)
- Start over: another $5,000–$8,000 in recruiting costs
```

Revised:
```
- 6–8 weeks to hire and train (our experience at G&G)
- Gone inside 18 months — trades turnover is brutal
- Start over: another $5,000–$8,000 in recruiting costs (lowball)
```

**2. Objection section — tighten the "task execution" repetition:**

**3. "Calls in sick" paragraph — tighten the list:**

Current: "A person shows up. A person answers the phone. But that person also calls in sick, gets poached by your competitor, and needs 6 weeks of training every time you scale."

Revised: "A person shows up. A person answers the phone. That same person calls in sick and walks out the door when your competitor offers fifty cents more an hour."
