## Pain Theme: Follow-Up Failure

### Strategic Position
Fred isn't losing jobs because his work is worse — he's losing them because he stops selling after one email, and his competitors don't.

### CFA Narrative
We run a $2M painting company. We know exactly what a quote folder full of forgotten leads feels like — because we lived it. The Follow-Up Machine runs a 30-day automated sequence on every single estimate: day 2, day 5, day 10, day 21, day 30. It doesn't get busy. It doesn't forget. In 30 days, it recovered $26K from estimates we had already written off. The system never forgets. Fred does.

### Hook Bank

1. **[Aggressive/Contrarian]**
"You didn't lose that job because the other guy is better. You lost it because he called back. You didn't."
**Rating: 9/10** — Passes all 4. Hits at 5am. Sounds like an operator. Not generic. Would absolutely come out of Andrew's mouth.

2. **[Proof-based — lead with the number]**
"$26,000. That's what was sitting in our quote folder doing nothing. We built a machine to go get it."
**Rating: 10/10** — Opens with a number that stops the scroll. Operator voice. Proof-first. Urgent because it implies ongoing loss.

3. **[Pain mirror — their exact words back at them]**
"'I quoted it and forgot to follow up.' You've said this. I've said this. The difference is I built something so I never have to say it again."
**Rating: 9/10** — Fred hears himself. Peer voice. Creates the gap between where he is and where CFA is. Passes all 4.

4. **[Urgency hook]**
"Every 48 hours that passes after a quote is sent, your close rate drops. Fast. Your competitor called yours this morning."
**Rating: 8/10** — Creates immediate clock pressure. Concrete mechanism. Slightly consultative on third sentence — punch it up: "Your competitor already called your prospect. Today."

5. **[Curiosity/loop hook]**
"We pulled 6 months of G&G follow-up data. What happened to every lead that didn't close in the first 48 hours will make you angry."
**Rating: 8/10** — Opens a loop. Operator-sourced. Passes 4-question test. Works best on YouTube and LinkedIn long-form.

6. **[Peer-to-peer hook]**
"I'm a painting contractor. I lose jobs when I forget to follow up. Here's what I built so my business stops depending on my memory."
**Rating: 9/10** — No distance between speaker and audience. Completely disarms the "vendor" perception. Passes all 4.

7. **[Results-first hook]**
"34% higher close rate. Same leads, same quotes, same prices. The only thing that changed: automated follow-up."
**Rating: 9/10** — Opens with outcome. Removes the "but my business is different" objection immediately. Tight and verifiable.

8. **[Pattern interrupt hook]**
"80% of sales need 5+ follow-ups. Contractors average 1.2. You're doing 1.2. The math isn't hard."
**Rating: 9/10** — Uses their industry data against them. Cold, factual, slightly confrontational — exactly right. Passes all 4. No fluff anywhere.

End with the mechanism (Follow-Up Machine), the result (34% close rate lift, $26K recovered), and a hard question back to the reader: "How many quotes do you have from the last 30 days you haven't touched?" Long-form post, 250–400 words. Intent: proof-driven conversion. Core/Casual audience.

Intent: educational proof. Casual/Core audience.

Target keywords: "contractor follow-up system," "how to follow up on contractor quotes," "contractor CRM follow-up." Structure: problem (Fred's exact language) → mechanism (the sequence) → proof (G&G numbers) → CTA (Assessment). 1,200–1,800 words. Intent: Casual audience trust-build, Assessment CTA.

### Archetype Target

- **Primary: Grind-Mode Gary** — He is the follow-up system. When he's busy, leads die. He has no mechanism between "quote sent" and "job won." This content hits him hardest because he knows it's true and feels the guilt.
- **Secondary: Scaling Sam** — Has a CRM but no one maintaining sequences. Intellectually knows this is a problem. Proof-based hooks land here because Sam responds to data, not just pain mirrors.

### Content Intent

**Aggressive + Proof-driven**
This brief serves Core and Casual CCN modes simultaneously. Core gets the hard proof and the direct CTA (they already trust CFA, they need a reason to act now). Casual gets the proof-based pain mirror — they recognize the problem, the G&G story validates that a real contractor fixed it, and the mechanism makes it feel achievable. Do not run this content cold — it presupposes enough context to know follow-up is the lever. New audience needs the pain mirror reels first.
